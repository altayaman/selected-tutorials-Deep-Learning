            Thanks! You can augment the training of your word embedddings with an auxiliary objective (e.g. to predict sentiment [1]). Pre-trained word embeddings are helpful for sentiment analysis as they capture general relations between words. However, they often cluster words of opposite sentiments (e.g. good and bad) together, so you always want to have a model on top of the pre-trained embeddings that can fine-tune the embeddings for your task.

                                  Sebastian Ruder Mod thecity2 a month ago

                                  Yeah, it's not as simple as just using the most similar vectors. You could learn embeddings for multi-word expressions (e.g. New York) by identifying them in a pre-processing step and treating them as a independent words (this is what Mikolov et al. do). Word2vec, however, is still best for generating representations of individual words. If you care about bigrams or longer n-grams, you're better off training a (neural or n-gram) language model on your corpus. Such a model can then easily provide you with the log-likelihoods for any n-gram sequence that you're interested in and generate new ones.
